NEW YORK (MarketWatch) -- Exxon Mobil Corp. said Monday its fourth-quarter net income fell 23% as the oil giant absorbed a loss in its refining operations, grew its revenue on higher oil prices and boosted production by increasing its spending on capital projects.

Exxon Mobil's XOM, +0.88% profit fell by about $1.77 billion to $6.05 billion, or $1.27 a share, from $7.82 billion, or $1.54 a share, in the year-ago period.

Revenue rose to $89.8 billion from $84.7 billion, as crude-oil prices climbed from year-ago levels and production rose.

Exxon Mobil beat the Wall Street analyst target -- as compiled by FactSet Research -- of $1.17 a share.

Shares of Exxon Mobil rose 2.7% to close at $66.18 on Monday.

Looking back at its fourth-quarter results, capital and exploration spending rose 21% to $8.3 billion.

Exxon Mobil's largest unit, the upstream business specializing in production of oil and natural gas, earned $5.78 billion, up by $146 million.

While higher crude-oil prices boosted its bottom line by $1.8 billion, lower natural-gas prices ate into profit by $1.2 billion. Reduced profits from asset sales also cut earnings by $600 million.

Exxon reported an average prices realization of $70.21 a barrel for oil during the fourth quarter, up 45% from $48.33 a barrel in the year-ago period. Average natural gas prices fell 24% to $4.23 per thousand cubic foot, from $5.57 in the year-ago period.

Meanwhile, Exxon Mobil's huge refining and marketing business swung to a fourth-quarter loss of $189 million, from a profit of $2.6 billion.

Exxon Mobil joined fellow Dow Jones Industrial Average component Chevron CVX, +0.06% in absorbing losses in the business of making gasoline and heating oil as the price of refined products failed to keep up with the rising cost of oil and a glut of supply faced the industry See Chevron earnings story.

Exxon Mobil CEO Rex Tillerson said the Irving, Texas, oil major faced "difficult global economic conditions" highlighted by "lower refining and fuels margins and lower natural-gas realizations [which were] were partly offset by higher crude-oil realizations."

Oil-equivalent production increased about 2%. Excluding the impacts of entitlement volumes, quotas and divestments, production rose more than 3%.

Exxon Mobil bought back $2 billion in stock during the fourth quarter, compared to $8 billion in share buybacks a year ago.

Looking back at all of 2009, Exxon Mobil's annual profit fell 56% to $19.42 billion, from $44.06 billion in 2008.

S&P Equity reiterated its strong buy rating on the oil giant. Analysts said Exxon Mobil's earnings of $1.27 a share beat their target by 14 cents a share, reflecting favorable tax items.

"Lower-than-expected oil production was offset by higher-than-expected natural gas volumes," S&P analysts said.

Exxon Mobil's fourth-quarter earnings update comes a month after the oil major proposed the largest merger in the energy business of 2009 -- with a $41 billion bid to buy XTO Energy XTO, +0.17% -- as the energy giant seeks to tap into unconventional natural-gas resources in the U.S.

Tillerson told members of Congress last month that wider use of hydraulic fracturing will help the United States increase its domestic-energy supplies, and help create jobs at home. See full story.

Shares of Exxon Mobil, the largest company by market cap in the Dow Jones Industrial Average, touched their 12-month high of 480.34 on Feb. 6, 2009. The stock fell sharply to its 52-week low of $62.22 a month later, on March 5, 2009.

Exxon Mobil finished out the first trading month of 2010 with a loss of about 5%, to the $65-a-share range.

With its eye on developing new energy projects around the world, Exxon Mobil on Jan. 25 signed an agreement with the Iraq Ministry of Oil to redevelop and expand the West Qurna-1 field in southern Iraq.

Exxon remains in discussions with the government on "other opportunities to assist Iraq in developing the country's resources."
